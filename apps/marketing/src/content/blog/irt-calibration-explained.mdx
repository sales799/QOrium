## The pass-rate trap

Most question banks label difficulty as easy, medium, or hard. The label is assigned by the author, eyeballed by a reviewer, and never updated again.

A question the author thinks is "medium" might be trivially easy for a 5-year backend engineer and impossibly hard for a fresh graduate. The label tells you nothing about the candidate.

## What IRT actually measures

Item Response Theory replaces the label with a number: the difficulty parameter (b). It sits on the same scale as candidate ability (θ). When b = θ, the candidate has a 50% chance of answering correctly.

That single number does more work than easy/medium/hard ever could. It lets you compare questions across roles, across formats, across time. A b = 1.2 Java concurrency question and a b = 1.2 React state-management question are, statistically, equally hard.

## The calibration loop

QOrium calibrates every question through a four-step loop:

1. **Seed estimation** — the author and two SMEs independently estimate difficulty. Their consensus becomes the prior.
2. **Pilot exposure** — the question is served to a calibration cohort (minimum 30 responses) alongside anchor items with known parameters.
3. **2PL fit** — we fit a two-parameter logistic model, estimating both difficulty (b) and discrimination (a). Questions with a < 0.4 are flagged for rewrite.
4. **Drift watch** — every 90 days, or after 500 new responses, we re-estimate. If b shifts by more than 0.3 logits, the question is pulled for review.

## Why this matters for your hiring

When every question has a calibrated difficulty:

- **Adaptive difficulty targeting** — serve a pack where 60% of questions sit within 0.5 logits of the candidate's estimated ability. The test is neither trivially easy nor demoralisingly hard.
- **Cross-role comparison** — compare a backend engineer's percentile against a data engineer's percentile on a common scale.
- **Fair rotation** — when anti-leak rotation swaps a question, the replacement must have a similar b value. Otherwise you change the test difficulty silently.

## The cost of skipping calibration

If you skip IRT, you are shipping untested content. Your pass rates fluctuate not because candidates changed, but because your content drifted.

Every QOrium question — ReadyBank, JD-Forge, or Stack-Vault — passes through this loop before it reaches a candidate. No exceptions.
