## POST TEXT (paste into LinkedIn)

A hiring manager called me last week. Genuinely upset.

"We just rejected a candidate after a 3-round technical interview. They scored 95% on our HackerRank screen. Then in the live interview, they couldn't explain a single answer they'd given. Every solution was clearly memorized."

"The HackerRank questions — we picked them from their library. So why did our screen lie to us?"

I had to be honest with him.

Public question libraries leak. Not in months. In days. A senior Java question that goes into HackerRank's library on Monday is on Reddit, Stack Overflow, GitHub, and three Indian recruiter Telegram groups by Friday. Candidates pre-coach. They memorize. The 95% becomes the 60% that the interviewer should have seen.

I tested 10 popular Java mid-senior questions sourced from public discussions. **Median time to find a verbatim answer via Google: 47 seconds. All 10 questions found.**

The cost of one bad senior-engineer hire — easily ₹50L+ in opportunity cost. The cost of the assessment that lied to us — ₹0.

You see the math.

This is exactly the problem QOrium is built to solve. Per-customer libraries. 24-hour rotation. Watermarking that lets us detect leaks within 24 hours of the first appearance. We sell it as the answer to "how do I trust an assessment score in 2026?"

It's expensive. ₹10L-1Cr+ per year for enterprise tiers. Compared to "free" public banks, it looks insane.

Until you do the math on the candidate who scored 95% and couldn't explain why.

We launch Talpro India as Customer Zero this month. First Bosch GCC discovery call is queued. If you're building or hiring in India and want a different approach — DM me.

— Bhaskar

## REPLY TEMPLATES

**For "but candidates should be able to use Google":** "Agreed for live coding. Disagreed for assessments where the score becomes the gate. If your screen says 95%, you assume technical depth. The screen should not be possible to cheat with a Google search."

**For HackerRank/Mettl employees pushing back:** "Genuine respect. The platforms aren't the villains here — they're solving the platform problem (rendering, scoring, anti-cheat infrastructure). The content is leaking faster than any one team can refresh it. We solve the content problem; you solve the platform problem. Different layers."

**For "are you saying all assessment data is unreliable?":** "Not all. I'm saying assessments built on public question banks have a structural reliability problem that gets worse over time. There are three solutions: (1) accept the noise and triangulate with interviews, (2) use a private library like Stack-Vault, (3) make the assessment itself contextual to the hiring decision (JD-Forge tier). All three are valid. None of them is 'free public bank with no rotation.'"
